What am I looking at?

This is a calculator that helps calculate the DIAAS value of a mix of food items. Make sure you've read the DIAAS information page first as we won't detail DIAAS basics in this documentation.

As a rule of thumb, all the proteins that come from animals contain enough of all of the indispensable amino acids but most of the proteins that come from plants don't. By choosing the right food items with the right proportions (to be consumed within the same meal), it is possible to balance the 9 indispensable amino acids when looking for protein sources in plants.

The calculator may be useful for:

  • flexitarians who want to reduce the carbon footprint related to their meat and dairy consumption.
  • vegetarians, vegans, athletes or people under caloric restriction who want to optimize their protein intake
  • chefs and industrialists who want to create amino acid balanced recipes
  • scientists looking for a centralized DIAAS database

Language

You can switch between French and English at the top-right of the application.

For now only food item names are translated.

Contextual help

Place the mouse over a number (PC) or click on it (touch screen devices and PC) to get contextual help.

Mixes

The first part of the application titled Diaas calculation for the current mix allows you to

  • load an example mix. Select one of the mix and click Load example mix
  • see the DIAAS value of the mix you are creating

You can use the pencil icon to edit:

  • the food weight in the mix (increase or decrease the quantity of that food in the mix). By default food items are added with a weight of 100g of product
  • the protein content in case what we have in our database doesn't match the product you have at home. Currently, this change will not persist for future mixes.

Listing food items, searching for food items

In the Food items section you can find the food items you'll add into your mix. You can also modify an example mix after loading it (changes will not persist for now)

When loading the page the first time, a search is automatically launched so that all the food items in our database are displayed.

You can enter a food item name and click the Search button to filter by name

Clicking the Clear name button will re-run the search with no filter on the food item name

You can also use one of the other filters:

  • Food type
  • Amino acid profile
    • all : no filter
    • high-quality: display only food items that are ranked "high quality" as per the DIAAS definition (DIAAS value >= 75)
    • aa+ : display food items that have a score > 100 for the given amino acid. For example you should typically:
      • filter with lys+ when trying to balance a grain (grains usually have lysine as a limiting amino acid)
      • filter with saa+ when trying to balance a legume (legumes usually have saa as a limiting amino acid)

Information about food items

To have more information, click the "View details" button:

  • Score type. Built as : <score-standard>-[<animal-model>]-<digestibility-model>. All the categories below are given from most precise to least precise data for human interpolation (see DIAAS information page for more details):

    • <score-standard> :

      • DIAAS : Digestible Indispensable Amino Acid Score
      • PDCAAS: Protein Digestibility Corrected Amino Acid Score. A few food items use this score when DIAAS data is not available. It is usually higher than the DIAAS.

    • <animal-model>. Only present for in-vivo studies.

      • human : experimentation with human subjects (rarely seen)
      • humanpredicted : Predicted from pig true ileal amino acid digestibility data based on the equations of Deglaire and Moughan et al. (2012). Used for example in this FAO document
      • pig
      • rats

    • <digestibility-model> (invivo). The explanation of the different models is out of scope of this documentation and you shouldn't worry about that. Just remember that using a different model may explain differences in the results when comparing studies and that they are given here from most precise to least precise.

      • TID : true ileal digestibility (sometimes called real ileal digestibility)
      • SID : standardized ileal digestibility
      • STTD : standardized total tract digestibility
      • TFPD : True fecal protein digestibility
      • AFD : Apparent Fecal digestibility

    • <digestibility-model> (invitro):

      • IVPD: In Vitro Protein Digestibility : studies conducted without any human or animal but only invitro.

  • Protein content : g of protein per 100g of food.

  • Food type
  • Cooked state for protein content : this is the cooked state related to the protein content figure provided. You should use it when you weight your ingredients. We almost always display the cooked state as crude to facilitate protein content comparison between food items. And it's more precise because weight may change for cooked grains and legumes depending on how long they were cooked for and how much water they absorbed.
  • Cooked state for DIAAS : this is the state of the product for which the DIAAS was evaluated in the study. For precise results, you should eat the product in that state. For example, if you're looking at the DIAAS of a flour: ideally the "Cooked state for protein content" should be given crude so that you can weight the flour before cooking it, and the Cooked state for DIAAS should be baked if you're going to bake the flour in an oven.
  • Reference : Link to the study that established the DIAAS value
  • Comment : any precision about the data provided.

DIAAS display

A color code is adopted to show which amino acids are missing:

  • DIAAS < 60 : red
  • 60 <= DIAAS < 75 : orange
  • 75 <= DIAAS <= 100 : yellow-green.
  • DIAAS > 100: green. Typically this is what's you'll be looking for in other food items when trying to balance a limiting amino acid.

Inside a thicker border is the value of the limiting amino acid (the lowest value of all 9 amino acids)

The BCAA names are displayed in bold. In bold and red is the leucine (the most anabolic BCAA)

For now, all DIAAS and PDCAAS scores are given

  • for the reference pattern of older children, adolescents and adults (> 3 years old)
  • computed with a protein nitrogen content of Nx6.25 (unless stated otherwise)
