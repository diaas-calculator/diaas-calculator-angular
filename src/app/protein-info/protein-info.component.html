How much protein should I eat?

This is a very much debated topic.

We'll try to sum up the various recommendations done by official entities or based on research.

They are expressed as grams of proteins per kg of body mass per day but there are also discussions about this. Some argue it should be based on lean body mass. Obviously if you are 20kg overweight, there is no reason you'd need more protein. For people who are overweight, I'd advise to apply one of the formulas for lean body mass or just use an estimate of their "ideal weight".

Protein combining

What is protein combining?

Protein combining or protein complementing is a method that aims at optimizing the biological value of protein intake, especially when consuming plant proteins.

As a rule of thumb:

  • Animal proteins do not need combining
  • Grains and nuts are usually deficient in lysine but have a lot of Methionine and cysteine (sulfur amino acids or SAA)
  • Legumes are deficient in SAA but have a lot of lysine

So the simplest way to combine plant proteins is to mix grains and nuts with legumes.

More info: see Protein combining made easy for vegetarians and flexitarians

Do I really need to worry about protein combining?

This is actually a topic open to debate. Organizations like the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics (AND) - formerly American Dietetic Association or (ADA) - have taken stances against the need of protein combining in vegetarian diets:

  • in 1993 they say in their Position Paper of the ADA on Vegetarian Diets : Plant sources of protein alone can provide adequate amounts of the essential and nonessential amino adds, assuming that dietary protein sources from plants are reasonably varied and that caloric intake is sufficient to meet energy needs. [...] Conscious combining of these foods within a given meal, as the complementary protein dictum suggests, is unnecessary

  • in 2016 they say in the Position of the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics: Vegetarian diets : Protein from a variety of plant foods, eaten during the course of a day, supplies enough of all indispensable (essential) amino acids when caloric requirements are met.

The rationale is that if you eat a variety of foods in sufficient quantity you'll get enough indispensable amino acids. The minor lack that may still be present will be balanced by the surplus of protein intake as compared to your daily requirement. For example 100g of protein with DIAAS 75 is basically the same as 75g of protein with DIAAS 100 so if you eat 20-30% more protein than your requirement, you'll be fine even with a DIAAS of 75.

If we dig a bit deeper : the 2016 statement refers to Mangels R, Messina V, Messina M. The Dietitian’s Guide to Vegetarian Diets. 3rd ed. Sudbury, MA: Jones and Bartlett; 2011

This book itself (Protein complementarity paragraph) refers to the below scientific sources :

  • 1: Young VR, Pellet PL. Plant proteins in relation to human protein and amino acid nutrition. Am J Clin Nutr, 1994; 59(5 Suppl): 1203S-1212S

  • 2: Mariotti F, Gardner CD. Dietary protein and amino acids in vegetarian diets - A review. Nutrients. 2019;11(11)

Those sources look at data for:

  • the typical energy required per day by age group
  • the protein requirement per day. For adults
    • (1) is based on 0.75g/kg/day (table 4)
    • (2) is based on the RDA of 0.8g/kg/day
  • the typical vegetarian diets generally observed

And they do calculations showing that the typical balanced vegetarian diet, including legumes, grains, fruits and vegetables is enough to provide all indispensable amino acids because on average it provides more than the daily protein requirement.

However:

  • it doesn't mean that protein combining is outdated: it just means that eating a variety of food items generally leads to good enough protein combined meals. For that matter, (1) states that The concentration of protein and the quality of the protein in some foods of vegetable origin may be too low to make them adequate, sole sources of proteins [...] Mixtures of plant protein foods may be of potentially high nutritional quality. [...] This concept of protein, or amino acid, complementation is pertinent to a discussion of plant foods.
  • if we go further than the global averages, (2) finds that in western countries vegetarians (EPIC-Oxford sample enriched with 2 more studies) 16.5% males and 8.1% females have an intake below the EAR of 0.66 g/kg, which is admittedly low.
  • also if you are aiming at more than 0.8 g/kg/day (see How much protein should I eat?) you may be hardly reaching your target. In this case, it may be helpful to look at protein combining in order to increase your proteins DIAAS without increasing the actual protein intake.

It may be worth quoting another recent experts consultation on that matter: in 2007, the AFSSA (French Agency for food safety) says in its Protein intake: dietary intake, quality, requirements and recommendations : Adult vegans may be advised to pay careful attention to ensuring their protein and energy requirements are covered and to use complementary protein sources

Do I need to combine proteins on a per meal or per day basis?

Short answer: it is probably enough to combine amino acids on a per day basis

Long answer:

A study on rat in 1947(1) found that : The formation of protein molecules is a coordinated tissue function and can be accomplished only when all amino acids which take part in the formation are present at the same time. The study concluded that 'incomplete' amino acid mixtures are not stored in the body, but are irreversibly further metabolized. Metabolized means that the amino acids are used for energy (rather than for building tissues)

However, more recent studies and done on human seem to indicate that we have the capability of storing lysine and maybe other amino acids for later use the same day. Authors of the Dietetician's guide to vegetarian diets wrote in 2022 (2):

These less-restrictive guidelines consider the contribution of the pool of IAAs that is maintained by the body [...] it has been estimated that within 3 hours following a protein-rich meal, a considerable amount of lysine is deposited in the intracellular spaces of the skeletal musculature [...] This essentially serves as a storage form of lysine that can be used for protein synthesis if a meal comprising primarily of grains, which are low in lysine, is consumed later in the day

It's unclear if the same is true for other amino acids and for how long exactly the amino acids are stored.

Another study of interest on that matter(3) has found that

  • some 90 grams of endogenous protein per day flows through the digestive tract from the mouth to the terminal ileum in the adult human
    • Endogenous protein are proteins that originates from the body itself, not from the food being digested.
    • 90g is comparable with the quantity of protein that we get from food during the day
  • Several measures of the apparent reabsorption of gut endogenous protein have been made with estimates ranging from 60 % – 80 %
    • It means that more than half of these 90g of proteins originating from the body could have played a role in the protein combining with the rest of the food ingested during the last meal.

It doesn't say what amino acids those proteins were made of, or for how long they were stored. They originated at least from the meal before the one being digested.

References:

  • 1: E. Geiger (1947) "Experiments with delayed supplementation of incomplete amino acid mixtures", Journal of Nutrition 34: 97 to 111
  • 2: R. Mangels, V. Messina, M. Messina "The dietetician's guide to Vegetarian Diets Issues and applications fourth edition" 2022
  • 3: Paul J. Moughan and Shane M. Rutherfurd Gut luminal endogenous protein: Implications for the determination of ileal amino acid digestibility in humans, 01 August 2012
