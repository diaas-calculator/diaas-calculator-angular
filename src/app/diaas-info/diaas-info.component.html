What is the DIAAS?

In a nutshell

DIAAS or Digestible Indispensable Amino Acid Score is a measure, experimentally determined, that provides an estimation for a given food about how much of each indispensable amino acid (or AA) will be assimilated by a human subject if ingested compared to it's needs.

Indispensable amino acids

Amino acids are basic elements contained in proteins. There are two kinds of amino acids

  • Dispensable amino acids: those can be synthetized by the body
  • Indispensable amino acids: we cannot synthesize them, they must be brought by the food.

There are 9 indispensable amino acids:

  • His: histidine
  • Ile : isoleucine
  • Leu: leucine
  • Lys: lysine
  • Saa : sulfur amino acids (or Met + Cys: methionine and cysteine)
  • Aaa: aromatic amino acids (or Phe + Tyr: phenylalanine and tyrosine)
  • Thr: threonine
  • Trp: tryptophan
  • Val: valine

Digestibility

When computing the DIAAS, a factor is applied to the quantity of amino acid initially contained in the protein to take into account how much of the amino acid is actually digested and made available to the body.

This factor differ for each food item and each amino acid. It is determined experimentally.

Human needs and reference pattern

The human needs were estimated based on three age groups:

  • Infants: 0 -> 6 month
  • Children: 6 month -> 3 years
  • Older children, adolescents and adults : > 3 years

For example, the needs for the Infants is based on the amino acid content of breast milk that is made available to the body after digestion.

Scoring

Scoring each amino acid

The score is a set of 9 numbers, one for each indispensable amino acid, such as a score of 100 means that the quantity of amino acid in the product equals the requirement for the age reference. This score for each amino acid is sometimes refered to as DIAAR : Digestible Indispensable Amino Acid Ratio

Thus, by definition the DIAAR of breast milk for Infants is 100 for every indispensable amino acid.

Limiting amino acid

Out of the 9 numbers, we usually keep only the lowest number which is the DIAAS value of the product. The amino acid corresponding to this number is the limiting amino acid in the food item.

Only the lowest number is retained because the body needs all 9 indispensable amino acids to function and the lack of one can prevent some reactions to happen. However some bodily functions like muscle synthesis can benefit from a surplus of some amino acids (see discussions about branched-chain amino acids).

Example

Score of each amino acid in quick oats for the Older children, adolescents and adults age reference pattern:

his iso leu lys saa aaa thr trp val
118 110 103 67 177 184 140 104 107

Lysine has the lowest score so the DIAAS value in this case is 67.

The requirement for Lysine is 48mg/g of protein.

The lysine score for quick oats is 67 meaning that each gram of quick oats will provide an estimate of 32mg of lysine to the body after digestion (67% * 48 ).

Mixing food items

Several studies have shown that it's possible to improve the DIAAS of a given food item by mixing it with another food item. Example: DIAAS obtained from individual ingredients is additive in mixed meal

The resulting DIAAS for a mix of food items can be computed as the weighted average of each amino acid DIAAS value (the weighting factor being the content of protein per 100g of product)

DIAAS variations and other measuring systems

PER

PER or Protein efficiency ratio consists of evaluating the protein quality by observing the weight gain of rats fed with it.

Chemical score

The chemical score consists in looking at the content of each amino acid in the food item and comparing it to a reference protein (for example the egg protein). It doesn't account for the amino acids lost during the digestion.

PDCAAS

The PDCAAS or Protein Digestibility Corrected Amino Acid Score was adopted by the US FDA and the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations/World Health Organization (FAO/WHO) in 1993 : Recent developments in protein quality evaluation

It is taking into account the digestibility of the protein and comparing the quantity of amino acids made available to the body after digestion with a reference value for that amino acid.

Establishing the PDCAAS for a product requires taking measurements during in-vivo experimentations.

Note that the computation of the PDCAAS involves a hypothesis on protein nitrogen content. Two values may be used, N*5.30 or N*6.25. Which one should be used depends on the type of food item and which regulatory entity's advise you follow (see discussion paragraph in this study)

To simplify things, as we know that the PDCAAS tends to over-evaluates the scores compared to the DIAAS, we'll always use N*6.25 which gives the lowest score.

DIAAS

The DIAAS was proposed by the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) in 2011: Dietary protein quality evaluation in human nutrition.

The differences with the PDCAAS are:

  • The PDCAAS uses the faecal digestibility while the DIAAS considers the ileal digestibility. Using faecal digestibility tends to overestimate the score.
  • The PDCAAS experimentation protocol that determines the digestibility involves rats while the DIAAS advises to use pigs preferably as the pig digestive system is closer to the human's system.
  • The PDCAAS considers the global digestibility of the product's protein (a single figure) while the DIAAS accounts for a specific digestibility factor for each indispensable amino acid
  • The reference values for the PDCAAS are based on a unique age group, the 2- to 5-year-old child which is deemed to be the more demanding. The DIAAS provides values for three different age groups, with more up to date data about human needs. Note that sometimes authors publish the PDCAAS for other age groups (it is just a multiplying factor to apply for each amino acid). In the calculator, we convert PDCAAS scores to the DIAAS reference pattern for older children, adolescents and adults (> 3 years old) for an easier comparison with DIAAS scores. More information about the conversions: see readme on github

Score types in the app

You'll find below the naming that we use in the app to describe what kind of score each food item represents depending on the study protocol.

The score type is built as : <score-standard>-[<animal-model>]-<digestibility-model>.

Example : DIAAS-rat-TFPD

The special types DIAAS-proxy and PDCAAS-proxy mean that digestibility data was missing in the literature for that food item and we have used a similar food instead. See the experts comments in the app for details in this case.

All the categories below are given from most precise to least precise data for human interpolation

  • <score-standard> :

    • DIAAS : Digestible Indispensable Amino Acid Score
    • PDCAAS: Protein Digestibility Corrected Amino Acid Score. A few food items use this score when DIAAS data is not available. It is usually higher than the DIAAS.

  • <animal-model>. Only present for in-vivo studies.

    • human : experimentation with human subjects (rarely seen)
    • humanpredicted : Predicted from pig true ileal amino acid digestibility data based on the equations of Deglaire and Moughan et al. (2012). Used for example in this FAO document
    • pig
    • rats

  • <digestibility-model> (invivo case). The explanation of the different models is out of scope of this documentation and you shouldn't worry about that. Just remember that using a different model may explain differences in the results when comparing studies and that they are given here from most precise to least precise.

    • TID : true ileal digestibility (sometimes called real ileal digestibility)
    • SID : standardized ileal digestibility
    • STTD : standardized total tract digestibility
    • TFPD : True fecal protein digestibility
    • AFD : Apparent Fecal digestibility

  • <digestibility-model> (invitro case):

    • IVPD: In Vitro Protein Digestibility : studies conducted without any human or animal but only invitro.

Branched-chain amino acids

Some amino acids are known to trigger an anabolic response, meaning that they induce muscle synthesis after resistance training. This is especially the case of leucine. And also to a lesser extent isoleucine and valine

Those amino acids are called the branched-chain amino acids or BCAA .

This study for example showed that a low-protein (6.25 g) mixed macronutrient beverage can be as effective as a high-protein dose (25 g) at stimulating increased MPS (myofibrillar protein synthesis) rates when supplemented with a high (5.0 g total leucine) amount of leucine

However it should be noted that BCAA alone are not enough to trigger the optimal response as concluded by this meta-study : Existing evidence suggests BCAA stimulate muscle protein synthesis following resistance physical exercise. However, in the absence of other essential amino acids, BCAA are not able to sustain maximal synthesis responses.

To sum up, we can say that muscle synthesis requires a sufficient amount of all the indispensable amino acid to be optimal, but can also benefit from higher levels of BCAA compared to the other indispensable amino acids.

Limitations and precautions

The DIAAS is never an exact value as many factors may impact the result.

  • Parameters that are out of our control

    • Difference between the pig digestive system and the human digestive system

    • Difference between each individual digestive system which can vary depending on our genes and our eating habits

    • Difference between products (varietal difference of the crops/ breed)

  • Parameters that we can control
    • Preparation of the food:
      • Is it crude, cooked or baked? Cooking/baking can increase or lower the DIAAS depending on the product
        • Is it soaked, or germinated before cooking? Those kinds of processing may improves the DIAAS of grains/legumes by reducing the quantity of some anti-nutrients
        • Is is extruded? The extrusion of grains and cereals may improve the DIAAS.
        • Did it go through a specific processing that our food doesn't usually go through?

Unfortunately many studies

  • don't describe enough the food preparation
  • or use a very specific preparation instead of using the food as we usually eat it (use of unfatted food, use of protein extraction processes...)
  • or study a crude food for a product that we only consume cooked
